Missouri United States Senate election, 2006

The Missouri Senate election of 2006 will be held on November 7, 2006. Whoever is elected will serve between January 3, 2007 and January 3, 2013.

The incumbent is Republican Jim Talent. Talent was elected in a special election in 2002 when he narrowly defeated incumbent Democrat Jean Carnahan. Carnahan had been appointed to the Senate seat following the posthumous election of her husband Mel Carnahan, who had died in a plane crash shortly before the 2000 election. Talent's Democratic opponent is Missouri State Auditor Claire McCaskill.

Missouri was seen the nation's bellwether state throughout the twentieth century. It has voted for the winner of every presidential election since 1900 apart from in 1956, when it narrowly bucked the trend in favour of Dwight D. Eisenhower's re-election and voted for Adlai Stevenson, who had the crucial farmer's vote; Missouri had actually voted for Eisenhower in 1952. Missouri's bellwether status was due to the fact that it not only voted for the electoral victor, but that its returns usually mirrored national returns.

The state itself is a central state, bordered by both the edges of Southern and Midwestern regions. In statewide contests for much of the twentieth century, Missouri favoured the Democratic party. In recent elections, the GOP has emerged in statewide contests. The election of 2004 was an important one; as George W. Bush was re-elected he carried Missouri. But this time his margin in the state was greater than it was nationwide. Bush won the Presidency 51% to 48%, he carried Missouri 53% to 46%. This trend had begun in 2000, when Bush lost the national popular vote to Al Gore 47% to 48% but still won Missouri, 50% to 47%. Bush's victory also saw Republicans triumph in several statewide contests; Senator Kit Bond was re-elected by a decisive 56% to 43% margin and Matt Blunt won the election for Governor, narrowly defeating state auditor Claire McCaskill 51% to 48%. The GOP also captured control of the state legislature for the first time in eighty years.

The election is expected to be very close, which seems fitting for a seat that has changed hands twice, both by very narrow margins, within the last six years. In 2000, the late Missouri Governor Mel Carnahan, a Democrat, narrowly defeated incumbent Republican Senator John Ashcroft 50% to 48%. Two years later in a special election held for the seat, incumbent Senator Jean Carnahan lost an even closer election to then-Congressman Talent, 50% to 49%.

[edit]

Lead-up to the August 2006 primaries

Talent, anticipating a tough re-election battle and attempting to dissuade challengers, has accumulated a large campaign fund. [1] For most of 2005, he had no opposition. State Senator Chuck Graham had briefly entered the race early in the year, but dropped out soon after. However, on August 30, 2005 Democrat Claire McCaskill announced her intention to run for Talent's Senate seat.

McCaskill started with a large financial disadvantage, but she was also an experienced candidate with high name recognition. McCaskill had run two successful campaigns for state auditor. She was also a candidate for governor in 2004, where she defeated the incumbent Democratic Governor Bob Holden in the primary election but lost with 48% of the vote in the general election.

Both Talent and McCaskill face unknowns in their respective primaries on August 8, 2006. (The filing deadline was March 28, 2006.) [2]

Talent started statewide advertising on August 1, 2006, forcing some observers to suggest that Talent is on the ropes and therefore needs to shore up his image (damaged recently by his "flip-flopping" on stem cell research, his opposition to raising the minimum wage and a general feeling of antipathy from the body politic regarding his lack of notable achievements while in the Senate) and pull ahead in a statistical dead heat. (Normally, unless there is a heated primary battle candidates do not start ads until after they have won the right to compete in the general election.)

McCaskill and Talent agreed to debate each other on Meet the Press on October 8, 2006. [3]

[edit]

November ballot propositions

A June 19-22, 2006 Research 2000 poll shows Talent's favorability rating is 47%-46%, with 7% having no opinion. Soon after a St. Louis Post-Dispatch poll was released showing McCaskill with 49% favorability to Talent's 43%.

It is believed that statewide ballot issues will drive the November 2006 vote, with Talent being on the opposite of the majority of voters in this poll on just about every issue: 66% of Missouri voters favor raising the minimum wage to $6.50 an hour; 62% of Missouri voters favor raising taxes to replace Medicaid funding cut by the current Republican Governor, Matt Blunt; 54% oppose a law that will require all Missourians to show a photo ID before they vote; 58% favor campaign donation limitations; and 66% favor restoring Medicaid coverage to about 90,000 Missourians who lost coverage when Blunt and the Republican legislature tightened eligibility requirements.

Perhaps most importantly, 62% favor a ballot proposal that would allow all types of stem cell research allowed under federal law - a measure Talent recently announced that he is against.[4]

[edit]

Outcome

The Missouri contest is seen as vitally important to control of the United States Senate; as a toss-up election between two strong candidates, the race is expected to attract a lot of interest as well as money spent on ads and turning out supporters. If Talent does win, then a Democratic takeover of the U.S. Senate becomes almost impossible; the Democrats' need to win six seats to take control of the chamber with 51 seats. To do this, they would need to retain their 19 incumbent seats and win the competitive Republican-held seats of Missouri, Montana, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Rhode Island. In addition, they would also need to win one other seat; Arizona, Nevada, Tennessee and Virginia have all been shown to be somewhat winnable for the Democrats. Currently, this vital "sixth seat" appears most likely to be Tennessee, although recent missteps by incumbent Virginia Senator George Felix Allen have made that election significantly more competitive.
