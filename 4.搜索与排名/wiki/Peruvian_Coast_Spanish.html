Peruvian Coast Spanish

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Jump to: navigation, search

Peruvian Coast Spanish is the form of the Spanish language spoken in the coastal region of Peru.

The Spanish spoken in Coastal Peru has three characteristic forms of speech. The first one is that of the Limean inhabitants of the near area to the sea and south, the Central or Lima form whose origin was in the old section of the city, from where it extended to the whole coastal region. The second is its provincial immigrant sociolect, and the third is its northern variant, almost extinct. In the southern section of the coast, the speech is related more to the Andean dialect.

[edit]

Historical Review

Between 1535 and 1739, Lima was the capital of the Spanish Empire in South America, from which the culture spread, and its speech became the purest because it was the home of the famous University of San Marcos of Lima. Also, it was the city that had the highest number of titles of nobility from Castile outside of Spain. Colonial people in Lima became used to living with an ostentation and courtly life style that the other capital cities of Spanish America lacked, with the exception of Mexico City and later the city of Bogotá. On the other hand, they lived for the richness extracted from the inland mines by the indians.

[edit]

Phonetics and phonology

  • The Lima accent does not have a strong intonation as the rest of the Spanish-speaking world does. (Some scholars believe that it is because of climate factors; young people tend to speak too fast, and the lower class and outcast boys' tonal curve is from Andean or remote Black origin).
  • In Lima there is no loss of syllable-final /s/ before a stop or a vowel. It is only aspirated in a preconsonantal position. This is unique, by all the social classes in the whole Latin American coast. The pronunciation of ese is soft predorsal.
  • There is a clear (but soft) emission of the vibrants /rr/ and /r/. In syllable-final position is never assibilated like Chile, Mexico or the Andes.
  • The so-called yeismo and seseo occur.
  • There is no confusion of /r/ with /l/ in syllable-final position like the Caribbean countries and the lower sociolects of Chile.
  • The letters 'j' and 'g 'before 'e' and 'i' are pronounced as a soft palatal [ç]. The jota is velar: /x/ (resembled Castilian) in emphatic or grumpy speech, especially before 'a', 'o' and 'u'. It is never /h/.
  • Word-final /d/ is usually unvoiced or turned to /t/.
  • Word-final /n/ is routinely velarized (the most highlighted Andalusian trait).
[edit]

Grammatical subject

Since the use of 'vos' instead of 'tú' like familiar form of address was rather of the lower class in Spain, it was never used in Lima.

The proper Limean speech is adjusted a lot to the standard (Spanish American) linguistic norm, this is due to the city's lack of contact with the Andean world and the autochthonous languages for centuries.

The speech of the coast of Peru, however, had its northern variant until the beginning of the 20th century, in which similarities were perceived with that of the Ecuadorian-Colombian coast. The most remarkable was the "vos" employment in the family treatment, which is completely missing today. This part of Northern Peru also had a strong influence of the Muchik or Mochica language, which is currently extinguished.

[edit]

Inland Immigrants Variation

The other main variety of Spanish from the coast of Peru is that which appeared after the penetration of the linguistic habits of the Sierra and of the rural environment into the coastal cities and the former 'Garden City' by the Great Andean Invasion (1940-1980).

This language could be catalogued as undereducated, and is the form which is spoken today by the youth and the great majority of residents of the capital.

Its main characterisitics are:

  • The strong use of diminutives, double possessives and the routinely use of 'pues' and 'nomás' in postverbal position.
  • The redudant use of verbal clitics, particularly 'lo' (the so-called loismo)
  • The bilabization of /f/
  • Closed timbre
[edit]

Recent Changes

The next sounds tend to debilitate:

  • /s/, in some guys, tends to [θ].
  • Intervocalic /b/ /d/ and /g/, among younger people.
  • Intervocalic /y/, by northern influence. In the lower class is generalized.

This popular variety of Coastal Peruvian dialect is not only nurtured by Andean influences but also, of course, by foreigners: Anglicisms, Mexicanisms and Argentinisms in the lexical level.

Finally, young people belonging to the highest economic stratum in Lima have also developed, mainly in the tonal aspect, certain peculiar and mannered form of speaking.

Some Common Expressions

  • Agarrar y + to do something (Agarré y le dije...).
  • Parar (en) = to be frequently in some place or to do something (Paras en la cabina).
  • De repente = perhaps.

Some traditional voices of the Limean speech

  • Anticucho = typical food consisting almost always of grilled chicken or cow heart.
  • Disforzarse = to be anxious. (the children)
  • Cachaco = soldier.
  • Calato = nude.
  • Chicotazo = lash.
  • Fresco/a (or conchudo/a)=shameless person.
  • Fregar = to bother, to ruin.
  • Gallinazo = typical fowl.
  • Garúa = tenuous rain.
  • Guachafo = ridiculous, gaudy.
  • Jarana = party.
  • Panteón = cemetery.
  • Penar = to roam in some house after dying.
  • Pericote = mouse.
  • Zamparse = to be introduced abruptly in a place.

Some informal words of extended use

  • Aguantar = to wait, to resist.
  • Chibolo/a = child, adolescent.
  • Paltearse = to be astounded, to be embarrassed.
  • Pata = male friend, guy.
  • Pollada = chicken food.
