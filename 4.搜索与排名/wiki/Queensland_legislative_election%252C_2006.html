Elections for the Queensland Legislative Assembly were held on September 9, 2006, after being announced by Premier Peter Beattie on 15 August 2006.

The election saw the incumbent Labor government led by Premier Peter Beattie defeat the National-Liberal Coalition lead by Lawrence Springborg and Bruce Flegg respectively, and gain a fourth consecutive term in office. With nearly three-quarters of of the vote counted, Labor seems likely to retain 50 or 60 seats in the 89-seat parliament, a drop of three or four from the 63 seats won in 2004. All three major parties, plus the Australian Greens, saw their vote rise slightly as a result of the disappearance of the Australian Democrats and the near-disappearance of One Nation.[1]

The day after the elections, it appeared that only three seats had definitely changed hands. The Liberals had gained Kawana and Noosa from Labor, while the Nationals had gained Gympie from an independent. It seemed likely, however, that Labor would also lose Clayfield to the Liberals, and it was possible that Labor would lose Bundaberg to the Nationals.

Beattie thus became the first Labor Premier of Queensland to win four consecutive elections since William Forgan Smith in the 1930s. If Beattie completed his term, he would be the second-longest serving Queensland Premier, after Sir Joh Bjelke-Petersen. The demise of the once dominant Nationals, who ruled Queensland from 1957 to 1989, was symbolised by the failure of Bjelke-Petersen's son, John Bjelke-Petersen, to win his father's old seat of Nanango from the independent member Dorothy Pratt.

[edit]

State of the parties

Since April 2006, the ALP holds 60 of the 89 seats in the Legislative Assembly, the Coalition 23 seats (16 National and seven Liberal), along with five Independents and one member of the One Nation Party. Thus in order to win an outright majority (45 seats), the Coalition must win an additional 22 seats from the ALP, the Independents or One Nation, assuming that they retain all of their own seats. This would require a uniform swing against Labor of approximately 8% (such swings are very rare).

Sitting Labor member for Noosa, Cate Molloy, has resigned from the Labor Party following her disendorsement as a Labor candidate, which in turn followed her repudiation of the state government's plans to build a dam on the Mary River at Traveston. Molloy is recontesting the seat as an Independent.

[edit]

Pre-Electoral issues

As Queensland is currently suffering a major drought, water management issues have become a major concern. The Coalition has contended that the Beattie government's water management strategies are inadequate. The government has in turn criticised the Coalition for its policies on water. Proposals to alleviate the water burden, such as the construction of a "mega-dam" at Traveston and the introduction of recycled water into the water supply of Toowoomba, have led to debate and in some cases embarassment for the government.

From mid-2005, after the revelation of the Jayant Patel scandal, the issue of health has become a focus of controversy, damaging to the Beattie government. After several inquiries and industrial disputes, a restructure of Queensland Health took place, and the state government is currently lobbying the federal goverment for more doctor training places in universities for Queensland. Critics of the state government allege that Queensland Health is still poorly managed.[citation needed]

Other issues of importance to the electorate include environmental management and land clearing, asbestos in state schools, the provision of transportation and infrastructure to rural and regional areas, and the management of South East Queensland's population growth.

[edit]

Campaign

The campaign started unusually with Premier Peter Beattie denying a general election was about to be called, while residents in some Gold Coast electorates received direct mail from the ALP stating that the election had been called for September.

At a press conference on 16 August, Liberal leader Bruce Flegg stated that in the event that the Coalition won government, and the Liberal Party won more seats than the Nationals, Lawrence Springborg would still become Premier [2]. Other Liberal Party MP's such as Michael Caltabiano disagreed, as this ran contrary to the coalition agreement signed between the two parties, which stated that whichever party won the most seats would form government. The ALP used this to attack Coalition stability in media and advertising.

Flegg was subsequently asked to leave a shopping centre in the Redcliffe suburb of Kippa-Ring for failing to obtain permission to do a campaign walkthrough. [3]. Flegg later denied that he had in fact been evicted.

On 22 August, Flegg took part in a media conference with Julie Bishop, federal Liberal Party Minister for Education, where he endorsed a Federal Government plan for the mandatory teaching of Australian history in schools. Responding to questions from journalists, he failed to identify the date of arrival of the Second Fleet (1790), or the person after whom Brisbane was named (noted astronomer and Governor of New South Wales Sir Thomas Brisbane) [4].

Two sad twists of fate impacted the 26-day campaign - on 30 August, opposition leader Lawrence Springborg took temporary leave from the campaign after the sudden death of his father-in-law, and National Party deputy leader Jeff Seeney and Liberal leader Bruce Flegg continued the campaign in his absence.[5] The death of TV personality Steve Irwin ("The Crocodile Hunter") on 4 September in an accident off Port Douglas, Queensland, took the media's focus away from the election in its final week. [6]

Current Treasurer Anna Bligh has stated the coalition's major election promises of wiping out stamp duty within five years, increasing the first home buyers grant by $3,000 and introducing a 10% per litre subsidy on ethanol-blended petrol will cost $2.4 billion dollars and has blown the budget. Lawrence Springborg says all his election promises are costed and affordable, with costings to be released two days before the election.[7] So far these costings have not been released.

On Friday September 8, the day before the election, Premier Beattie and Opposition Leader Springborg participated in a "great debate". [8] at the Brisbane Convention & Exhibition Centre, moderated by ABC journalist Chris O'Brien.

Although Newspoll and other published polls showed Labor well ahead on predicted two-party-preferred vote, Labor strategists feared that people would vote for the Coalition in a protest vote, expecting Beattie not to lose. [9] They adopted a strategy of denying Labor was in fact ahead. [10] The Roy Morgan poll suggested the Liberal vote had fallen, while the Greens had risen to 8%.[11]

[edit]

Results

With 73.5% of the primary vote counted, the result appears largely unchanged from the 2004 election. The Electoral Commission of Queensland, in its final release on election night, indicated the ALP was ahead in 60 seats, Liberals in 9, Nationals in 16, One Nation in one (Tablelands) and independents in three. [12] Lawrence Springborg has conceded the election. [13]

It appears Labor have reversed their by-election losses - they have regained Gaven and Redcliffe and are ahead in Chatsworth - and are within 1% of winning Gladstone (IND) and Robina (LIB). Bundaberg and Clayfield (ALP-held) and Lockyer (NPA) are too close to call at this stage.

Nanango independent Dorothy (Dolly) Pratt looks safe against the Nationals' John Bjelke-Petersen. Labor's Chris Cummins, a minister in the Beattie government, has conceded defeat in his marginal Sunshine Coast seat of Kawana, while Gympie independent Elisa Roberts has conceded to the Nationals.

In Noosa, where the Government has come under fire because of its plans to dam the Mary River, the Liberal Party's Glen Elmes looks set for victory.
