Lakewood Church

Lakewood Church is a megachurch located in Houston, Texas.

The church currently has three English services and one Spanish service each weekend.

[edit]

The Beginning

John Osteen, a former Southern Baptist minister who adopted the teachings of the Charismatic movement, started Lakewood on Mother's Day in 1959 with his wife Dolores (Dodie), a nurse, inside an abandoned feed store in northeast Houston. The area was transitioning into a predominantly African-American neighborhood, and from the beginning Lakewood would be a highly integrated congregation. Lakewood would continue to grow and expand at its northeast Houston location through 1999.


[edit]

Television Ministry

Joel Osteen, John and Dolores' youngest son, provided technical skills and stayed behind the cameras, never having a desire to preach. He briefly attended Oral Roberts University for about a year and a half, but chose to return to Lakewood with a vision for expanding Lakewood's television ministry far beyond the one service broadcast on a local Houston channel. John agreed to put up the funds for Joel's vision, with one stipulation: Lakewood would never solicit money from viewers on its broadcasts. (from Texas Monthly article, see below)

Church services are televised and are aired weekly in over 140 countries, and the Internet. Lakewood broadcasts multiple times on Trinity Broadcasting Network and Daystar Television Network, as well as local channels in most major markets. Lakewood also appears on "secular networks" such as Black Entertainment Television and USA Network. Lakewood does not produce any shows other than re-broadcasts of its services. Its one-hour show features singing and the sermon; the 30-minute show omits the singing and features only the sermon.

By January 1999, John Osteen's health had deteriorated significantly to the point where he could no longer preach. He asked Joel to fill in for him one Sunday morning.

Joel, having never preached, was not interested. He was quite happy behind the scenes running Lakewood's growing media ministry. However, he reluctantly agreed to do so. Afterwards, he thought it was a "colossal failure" and waited for his father to return to full-time preaching. However, it would never happen. On January 23, 1999 (less than one week after Joel's first sermon), John Osteen died at age 77. In October 1999, Joel Osteen would become the new senior pastor.

The congregation continued growing. Even adding multiple services was not enough to accommodate the ever increasing crowds. And the campus was now landlocked, so no further expansion was possible. Church leaders started looking for properties to build a new facility for worship, but the task was difficult.

About this time, the Compaq Center (former home of the NBA Houston Rockets) became available for lease. Having rented Compaq Center for special events, Joel realized that it would be perfect. Lakewood submitted an offer to lease the facility and, (after criticisms regarding "separation of church and state" were resolved), Lakewood agreed to pay $12.1 million up front for a 30-year lease (the amount has been reported elsewhere as $11.8 million and $12.8 million), with an option for an additional 30 years at $22.6 million. In addition, Lakewood is currently investing over $95 million in facility improvements. The new facility (Lakewood Church, Central Campus) opened in July 2005.

The church's original campus (Northeast) remained as the church and media ministry offices until the end of 2005 but has been sold to another church organization which is now holding services there.

Beginning in July 2004, Joel Osteen Ministries and Lakewood Church began traveling to cities across America putting on "An Evening With Joel Osteen". Tens of thousands have gathered for these events which are very similar to Lakewood's Sunday morning worship services which feature upbeat worship lead by Cindy Cruse-Ratcliff, time of encouragement from Dodie Osteen and Joel's wife Victoria, and a sermon from Pastor Joel.

[edit]

Criticisms

As a charismatic/Pentecostal church, Lakewood is subject to regular criticism regarding its views on faith healing and the prosperity gospel. In addition, other criticisms of Lakewood and Joel Osteen have included: 1) a style of preaching that avoids theology or controversial topics (referred to in some circles as "Christianity lite" or "cotton candy Christianity"). The church even goes as far as to avoid the use of crosses or providing Bibles (except for in the gift shop); 2) a board of directors consisting completely of Osteen family members (this disqualifies Lakewood from becoming a member of the ECFA); and 3) the Osteen's lifestyle (Joel and his family live in a multi-million-dollar home in an exclusive Houston neighborhood, and travel first class to speaking engagements, while other Osteen family members also live comfortably).

With regard to the above comments, supporters of Osteen and Lakewood note the following:

  • Although John Osteen did die from his illnesses, Dodie Osteen reportedly has survived a bout with liver cancer.
  • Lakewood has never taught an extreme view of faith healing, where one is taught that seeking medical attention constitutes a "lack of faith". (John Osteen was in the hospital when he died, and Joel's older brother Paul was a medical doctor and surgeon for nearly 20 years before coming to work at Lakewood.)
  • Joel Osteen has declined to take a salary (last reported at $200,000) from Lakewood, due to the success of his best selling book, "Your Best Life Now". (Rick Warren also does not take a salary from his church due to successful book sales.) According to the August 2005 edition of Texas Monthly, Osteen has also paid property taxes on his house (which, legally, could be classified as a church parsonage, making it exempt from property tax).
  • Lakewood has deliberated about making its budget and financial statements public. In response to the inquiries of WXYZ-TV in Detroit, Michigan and Texas Monthly (both of which did reports on Joel Osteen and Lakewood), Lakewood provided two years of audited financial statements to the reporters.

Ole Anthony and Trinity Foundation have investigated Lakewood and Joel Osteen. Anthony stated that his investigation has turned up no evidence of financial or moral impropriety, and that the differences are confined to financial and doctrinal interpretations of the Bible.

Lakewood Church did not open its door to refugees following the Hurricane Katrina disaster due to space and logistical limitations. Instead, the church sent members to help out at the Astrodome complex and with disaster relief organizations. Lakewood also collected millions of dollars from its members as well as multiple truckloads of supplies for Katrina victims. It dedicated an entire service to the prayer and support of those victims. After that service, Lakewood volunteers provided an opportunity for them to speak with area care providers and gather information on employment and housing.
